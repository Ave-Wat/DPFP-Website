Polarization and the Extremes: QAnon and Antifa

By Claire Boettcher and Karli Tellis

Introduction

Political polarization is running rampant in the United States of America, fuelled by fear and hatred as it threatens to unravel American democracy. Research suggests that political elites have been becoming more polarized over time, but continued studies are highlighting a phenomenon that is even more concerning: the American public is becoming more polarized as well. Being a member of a political party is analogous to rooting for a sports team- we want our side to win, and, perhaps more importantly, we will do whatever we can to see our opposition lose. This phenomenon of harboring animosity towards the opposing party, known as affective polarization, is destroying our chances at compromise, communication, and change. We see the implications of polarization in our daily lives, and social media isn’t helping, stoking the flames of political passion and allowing people to organize themselves into like-minded groups online. These online communities are allowing people to further polarize one another, taking their ideas to the extremes and, eventually, turning their thoughts into reality. These groups of people with radical ideas dominate mass media, with liberals citing QAnon as the source of election conspiracy theories and causing the insurrection at the Capital, and conservatives blaming the effects of January 6th on Antifa spies. The stark divisions within our country are manifesting in extremist groups that have dominated mainstream media since 2017, but is the situation truly as dire as it seems?

We sought out to answer that question. As Fox News and other conservative media outlets paint groups like Antifa as the villain, liberal media such as CNN and MSNBC point to QAnon as the problem. Each extremist group is painted as the downfall of modern society, each seen by the opposition as the cause of civil unrest and the source of the country’s problems. QAnon

We aim to observe how parties, party members, and media outlets accept or reject extremists of their respective parties. Of course, the term “extremist” is thrown out quite frequently in this day and age, a catch-all for any person or group someone is trying to discredit. Thus, we define an extremist as someone whose ideas, policy proposals, or actions do not conform to the official platforms of either party; an individual or group who pushes the boundaries of their party to either the political right or political left; or a person or group who aligns father on the ideological spectrum than the average member of their party. In part, we hope to understand how media and parties respond to these extremist groups, as well as how party gatekeeping can affect the media landscape and can alter the narrative and perception of these groups.

QAnon and Antifa are seen as the villains of contemporary American society by liberals and conservatives, respectively. By assessing how each side assesses the “villain” associated with their side, we hope to gain insight into the political divisions within the country and understand how parties respond to attacks from the opposing party

In October of 2017, an anonymous account entitled “Q Clearance Patriot '' posted on the notoriously toxic message board known as 4chan. “Q” claimed to be a high-ranking government official with access to classified information about the Trump administration's war on ‘the cabal.’

1The centerpoint of QAnon ideology rests on the idea that a cabal of Satan worshipping pedophiles control a deep state government that seeks to undermine the Trump presidency, mostly through media and entertainment platforms. This secret society supposedly consists of elitist Democrats, journalists, entertainers, and other high-ranking figures that seek to sabotage the Trump presidency.2 Claims have been made that people such as Hillary Clinton, Barack Obama, Oprah, Tom Hanks, Ellen DeGeneres, and even Pope Francis and the Dalai Lama are all members of this elite organization. QAnon claims Donald Trump was recruited by top military generals to run for president in 2016 in order to destroy this cabal and bring its members to justice. Trump is waging a war of good against evil that will result in the arrest and execution of deep state officials and restore America to greatness.3 This reckoning is known as the Storm, and its arrival combined with the Great Awakening (in which all people recognize the truth of QAnon) will usher in a new era of American prosperity.3What used to be a fringe conspiracy in 2017 became a mainstream phenomenon in 2020.

QAnon supporters' used social media to spread fake information about covid-19, the Black Lives Matter movement, and the legitimacy of the 2020 election.This resulted in a surge of new followers and widespread notoriety. Q's wide membership base includes a wide array of people ranging from Trump supporters to yoga moms, from Evangelical Christians to Wall Street executives. This widespread support has resulted in many comparing QAnon to a huge multiplayer game or religion. Believers buy into an elaborate fantasy game of wars, codes, and secret societies, while Q offers explanations for problems or feelings people are facing.4 In December of 2020, more than one in three Americans believed that the Deep State was working to undermine the Trump Presidency, and QAnon membership increased over 10 times from January to August of 2021.57 QAnon supporters have been charged with violent crimes including assisantion plots and kidnappings, and were among the most prominent members of the January 6th insurrection at the Capitol.6 In light of QAnons increasing membership, let's analyze support for QAnon in Congress, the Republcian Party, and in the media.

Congress

QAnon has been met with mixed reactions in Congress, especially among the Republcian Party. Some Congress members have openly endorsed or opposed QAnon, while others have left their allegiance out of the public eye. Majorie Taylor Greene (Georgia) and Lauren Boebert (Colorado) are two notable QAnon supporters that joined the House of Representatives in 2020, but at least a dozen other Republican congressional candidates have endorsed or accepted the QAnon rhetoric of Trump as a guardian against the child-molesting democrats.7 Greene has referred to Q as a patriot, and said in 2017 that "There's a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity to take this global cabal of Satan-worshiping pedophiles out, and I think we have the president to do it." When asked about QAnons theories last May, Boebear said in an “I hope that this is real. It only means America is getting stronger and better, and people are returning to conservative values, and that’s what I am for.” However, both Greene and Boebert sought to separate themselves from QAnon after gaining a seat in the House. President Trump has spoken amicably about QAnon in the past, and even referred to Greene as a “future Republican star.” Most GOP Congressmen have not taken a hard stance on QAnon, but the campaign branch of the GOP has pushed QAnon theories in hopes of turning districts red.8 Republcian support for QAnon is visible but it is impossible to know the depth of support for Q’s theories and goals within the party.

While some Republican Congress members have embraced QAnon, others have vehemently opposed QAnons theories and the impact they have had on the Republcian party.

Several GOP members have spoken against QAnon and the damage it is causing to the party. Representative Peter Meijer told CNN that he believe the QAnon conspiracy theory movement could destroy the Republican Party from within, saying "The fact that a significant plurality, if not potentially a majority, of our voters have been deceived into this creation of an alternate reality could very well be an existential threat to the party." Rep. Adam Kinzinger is another GOP congressman who has publicly criticized the QAnon movement, forming a PAC aimed a fighting the rise of conspiracy theories in the Republcian Party and providing support for anti-Trump Republicans seeking to run for office.9 House Minority Leader Kevin McCarthy said that “There is no place for QAnon in the Republican Party'' on Fox News.10 Republican Senator Ben Sasse warned that QAnon was destroying the GOP in an op-ed last January, and following the insurrection on January 6th, several GOP lawmakers called for the party to distance itself from Trump's legacy.11 Many GOP lawmakers have condemned or criticized QAnons conspiracies and are working to limit its impact on the unity of the Republican Party and its reputation.

Party Members

QAnons growing support base has been felt most strongly in the Republican Party. According to a Daily Kos/Civiqs Poll done in September of 2020, 56% of Republicans believe that the QAnon theory is mostly true, while another 23% of Republicans and 4% of Democrats believe QAnon is only partly true. Only 13% of Republicans believe the theory is not true at all, while 72% of Democrats agree with this statement.12 One in five Americans, and 28% of Republicans, agree that there is a “storm coming soon,” that will “sweep away the elites in power and restore the rightful leaders.” 28% of Republicans also say that “because things have gotten so far off track” in the United States, “true American patriots may have to resort to violence” to save the country. 13According to a Ipsos/NPR poll done in December of 2020, more than 1 in 3 Americans (39%) believe a ‘Deep State’ is working to undermine Trump. This belief is driven primarily by Republicans and FOX News viewers, though nearly half of white men and rural residents (49% each) agree with this as well.14 Over 4 in 10 Americans who believe that far-right news outlets (such as OANN and Newsmax) are the most truthful subscribe to QAnon conspiracy theories.15 There are QAnon supporters present in both the Democratic and Republican Party, but the majority of Q’s base consists of self-identified Republicans.

Media

Depictions of QAnon in the media have varied over time and among media outlets. Many news outlets began introducing QAnon as a conspiracy group in 2020, and most left leaning news sites have continued to define them as such amidst their rise in popularity. Left leaning or centrist news sites (defined by the AllSides Media Bias Chart) such as the New York Times, the BBC, Wall Street Journal, Washington Post, NBC, the Atlantic, CBS, NPR, AP News, and a plethora of other news sources have published pieces labeling QAnon as a conspiracy theory and have continued to defined it as such as coverage has continued.16

The Atlanitc refers to QAnon as a dangerous new phase in American conspiracy theories, saying it is“a movement united in mass rejection of reason, objectivity, and other Enlightenment values.” 17 A CBS article discussing the evolution of QAnon explores some of the most popular QAnon theories such as the Deep State, the 9/11 ‘truther movement,’ anti-semtic claims surrounding ownership of world banks, and the ‘Save Our Children’' (from a secret elitist group of pedophiles and sextrafficers) campaign.18 The BBC introduces QAnon as a “wide-ranging, completely unfounded theory,” and draws attention to the group's often far-fetched conclusions.19 Liberal and centersit new sites continually define QAnon as a conspiracy theory/group and discredit the allegations and conspiracies the group endorses.

Left-leaning and centrist news sites have framed President Trump's comments about QAnon as praise for the group and its mission. When asked about QAnon in August of 2020, President Trump said that he did not know much about it other than that “they like me very much,’. He said QAnon members were people who love their country and were tired of seeing violence in major cities.20 President Trump has publicly refused to denounce QAnon as a conspiracy theory and has leaned into the rhetoric that QAnon members are patriots, an action heavily condemned by center/left news sites. Right-leaning news outlets such as Fox News or OAN had limited coverage of these statements and mainly focused on Trump saying that he did not know much about QAnon.

However, in June of 2020 Fox News published an article entitled ‘QAnon conspiracy theory enters politics: What to know.’ This article looks into the real world violence that has occurred as a result from QAnon rhetoric, like when the man charged with killing the boss of the Gambino crime family had a QAnon symbol written on his had during a court appearance, when a women inspired by QAnon sought to kidnap her own child, or when a QAnon believer attempted to hijack the Hoover Dam.21 It even features quotes from Will Sommer, a Daily Beast (a notable left news source) journalist who has been reporting on QAnon for three years, and mentions other notable Trump supporters that have condemned QAnon. Fox has released several other articles criticizing the way the liberal media equates QAnon with the Republican Party.22 Most written articles on QAnon by Fox News label them as a conspiracy group and attempt to distance QAnon from the GOP.

Fox News anchors on television have been more hesitant to classify QAnon as a conspiracy group. Talk show host Tucker Carlson has stated that he believes the Left invented QAnon, and that cable news and politicians are to blame for the false information being spread to Americans. Carlson has also defended QAnon by pushing against the idea that QAnon was a real threat in the weeks before January 6th. Many participants in the siege on the US Capitol have admitted to being QAnon believers, including some of the most prominent members of the crowd such as ‘QAnon Shawman’ Jake Angeli. 23 Carlson continued to defend QAnon after the insurrection by painting their actions as a act of freedom rather than treason, and on January 26th said

“There's a clear line between democracy and tyranny, between self-government and dictatorship. And here's what that line is. That line is your conscience. They cannot cross that. Government has every right to tell you what to do. No democratic government can ever tell you what to think. Your mind belongs to you, and it is yours and yours alone."24

One American News Network, or OAN (a right leaning organization), has also supported QAnon members. They have labeled any attempt to stop advocates for the January 6th insurrectionists as a cap on free speech, and even had QAnon themed emojis available on their YouTube Live streaming platform.25 Right-wing cable news channels have shown strong support for QAnon compared to their written counterparts.

Antifa

“Antifa” has become a buzzword in mainstream media, representing a militant and violent organization dedicated to chaos and destruction. Public figures like Donald Trump and Ted Cruz claim that Antifa is the enemy, the true villain of American politics, a terrorist organization devoted to destroying the country. But what is antifa, really?

Antifa, short for antifascist, is a left-wing movement that has been around, in some form, since the 1930’s, and has roots in the American punk scene. There is no centralized authority, and many (not all) of those who associate themselves with the movement are anarchists or anti-government, support defunding the police, with some believing that the system needs to be dismantled and totally rebuilt to ensure inclusivity and equity. But the big question still remains: Are they violent? And if they are, are they as violent as conservative media claims they are?

Antifa members are willing to use force and violence to achieve their goals: eradicating fascism in any and all forms. At their best, they are a scattered group of individuals willing to do what they must to fight hatred. At their worst, they are violent and counterproductive to their cause.

Because Antifa is left-wing and commonly portrayed as being associated with progressive liberals, far-right conservatives often attribute the work of Antifa as a manifestation of everything wrong with liberal ideology, making Antifa a scapegoat to paint liberals and the left as the enemy. How are liberals responding to this? Do they support what Antifa is doing, denounce it altogether, or remain neutral? The answer is mixed, and varies depending which media outlets you look at.

Media

Broadly, Antifa is portrayed in liberal media as less of a threat than conservatives make it out to be, arguing that its decentralization and lack of leadership makes it a movement unlikely to do any real or lasting damage to American government. Outlets such as the New Yorker and Vox, rated by AllSides as sites with a bias towards the left, take care to highlight the movement’s lack of organization and portray it more as an ideology aimed at eradicating facism and racism. Quick to denounce the violence that can erupt from protests with alleged Antifa involvement, they highlight the movement’s other tactics in achieving their goals and cite the lack of evidence implicating Antifa in acts of violence. While these liberal outlets do not appear to condone what members of Antifa are doing, there is a clear attempt to put out the flames of passion lit by conservatives (namely former President Donald Trump) and characterize Antifa not as a threat or terrorist organization, but a movement of disenfranchised citizens advocating for an ideology they believe in.

In an article for Vox in 2020, Zack Beauchamp writes ‘Antifa, explained’, detailing the movement’s origins and actions. The article opens with a restatement of conservative fear; “Donald Trump seems convinced… that antifa, a militant left-wing anarchist movement, is taking advantage of the [Black Lives Matter] demonstrations to burn the country down”26. The language used is strong and impassioned, highlighting what many fear. However, he goes on to say that Antifa is nothing like those terrorist groups. It is not a unified organization, but rather a loose ideological label of a subset of left-wing radicals who believe in using street-level force to prevent the rise of what they see as fascist movements. It is a kind of anarchist alternative to the police that originally took root in America’s punk scene, without any kind of national command or control structure” (emphasis added).27

Cleverly, the author diffuses some of the terror surrounding Antifa by painting it as a non-organization, a movement of ideas with no clear direction or leadership. Further, he points out that “there is no reason to believe that antifa is responsible for [the protests’] (occasional) turn towards violence”, arguing “Antifa members are not responsible for the unrest.”28 This deflection implies that there is another cause of the violence, something not being discussed by mainstream media. Indeed, Beauchamp says that Antifa “are a trumped-up boogeyman for the conservative movement, a totem used to justify their violent ‘law and order’ approach to legitimate demonstrations demanding racial justice.”29 Beauchamp flips the narrative here, describing conservatives as violent while maintaining that Antifa is not to blame, at least not fully, for the chaos ensuing at protests around the country. Emphasizing the movement’s decentralization and localization, Beauchamp continues to say that “the actual antifa movement is very small and, by all accounts, ancillary to the main story of the current waves of protests.”30

Interestingly, Beauchamp says that “[Antifa’s] activity, both violent and nonviolent, arguably helped curtail the spread of the alt-right.”31 While this does not outright condone the actions of Antifa, it speaks volumes that the author would give credit to a movement that is so controversial as doing something beneficial for the nation.

Additionally, Beauchamp notes that Antifa is not just violence and hatred, as it is made out to be. “Members of antifa groups do more conventional activism, flyer campaigns, and community organizing… This type of work… makes up the ‘vast majority’ of antifa activity.”32By highlighting the more acceptable and productive methods of achieving their goals, in combination with the lack of evidence attributing the violence and looting to Antifa, Beauchamp challenges common misconceptions about Antifa. In doing so, he is able to simultaneously appease the fear surrounding the idea of Antifa while suggesting that it is the far-right that is the true source of violence and chaos in the Nation.

CNN published an article in 2019 by Ganim and Welch entitled “Unmasking the leftist antifa movement”. Once more, liberal media takes care to highlight that Antifa is not nationally organized, but CNN takes it a step farther in articulating a positive goal for the movement; “Antifa activists, who operate without any centralized leadership, told CNN that their goal is peace and inclusivity. They often denounce capitalism and government” (emphasis added).33 Describing Antifa as activists with a goal of peace and inclusivity, both phrases with positive connotations, counters the common perception of the group. However, this positivity doesn’t last very long, as the authors go on to say that Antifa’s methods are “often violent”, with members “willing to physically attack anyone who employs violence against them or who condones racism- as long as force is used in the name of eradicating hatred.”34 Although CNN admits that members of Antifa use violence, their description of it as a “willingness” in the name of “eradicating hatred” is forgiving, and lends itself to a justification for the violence. They go on to say “Antifa activists often don’t hesitate to destroy property, which many see as the incarnation of unfair wealth distribution.”35 Once again, the authors seem to justify, to a certain extent, the activity of Antifa, while admitting that Antifa is responsible for property destruction. This justification continues: “Antifa members also sometimes launch attacks against people who aren’t physically attacking them. The movement… sees the alt-right hate speech as violent, and for that, its activists have opted to meet violence with violence.”36

The New Yorker published an article, “In the Streets with Antifa”, in late 2020, detailing what Antifa truly is, how it came to be, and what they do. The article centers around the events of September 26, 2020, in Portland, Oregon, where violence erupted as both right-wing and left-wing groups organized rallies and protests. The Antifa protest, as characterized by the New Yorker, was “family-friendly,” a description that isn’t typical of protests.37 This description, notably in the first paragraph of the article, sets up a view of Antifa protests counter to popular perception: that the event is something that anyone can attend, an environment conducive to all ages. This notion runs counter to Trump’s and traditionally conservative conceptions of these events; rather than being the site of America’s demise, they are family appropriate.

The author continues to battle misconceptions surrounding Antifa, stating that “most Americans who identify as antifascists don’t belong to any particular group, and there is no national Antifa hierarchy or leadership.”38 The author continues to suggest that perhaps it is the right-wing that is the problem; “...in response to such right-wing events some leftists have mobilized under the name Antifa, following the tradition with specific principles, among them a willingness to engage in violence.”39 Not only is the article claiming that Antifa is a response to far-right groups, but beginning to suggest that the members of this movement are not inherently violent, but rather they are willing to engage in violent acts to protect their beliefs and their communities. Mogelson goes on to highlight other routes Antifa, namely the Torch Antifa Network, takes to achieve their goals, saying “the network focuses much of its energy on Internet activism: doing research online, identifying proponents of bigotry, and then publicly exposing- or ‘doxing’- them.”3 According to the author and his sources, this movement is much more than just violence and destruction, but rather actively takes steps to stop hatred in its tracks.

However, there is no way to avoid the violence that tends to erupt when far-right and far-left groups clash. When confronted with a violent situation, Mogelson says “antifascist doctrine does not allow for avoiding such confrontations”(emphasis added).40 In Portland, a member of the far-right organization Patriot Prayer was shot and killed by a white Antifa supporter. The author, however, made sure to note that the shooter was “unaffiliated” with Antifa; the only connection between the group and the shooter was the shooter’s Instagram post claiming to support the work of Antifa.

In a rather disturbing description, Mogelson describes Patriot Prayer founder Joey Gibson being harassed as he was attempting to flee Portland:

“[Gibson] kept up a show of equanimity until his hat and glasses were snatched away. Soon, drinks were emptied on him, objects were hurled at him, eggs were smashed on him, and he was punched and pepper-sprayed… Someone rang a cowbell in his ears and others strobed flashlights in his eyes. ‘Kill the Nazi!’ someone screamed… As far as I could tell, all of Gibson’s assailants were white.”41

When Gibson sought asylum in a gas station, “people smashed the windows and kicked open a side door.”42 A Filipino-American who marched for the Black Lives Matter movement was there through it all, shocked at what he saw, and revealed to Mogelson that he “worried that in Portland [the Black Lives Matter movement] had been subsumed by the city’s militant antifascist culture, which he saw as violent and white.”43

Later, outside of the Portland Justice Center, a woman with a megaphone shouted to a crowd, “‘I just got word that the person who died was a Patriot Prayer person… He was a fucking Nazi. Our community held its own and took out the trash… I am not sad that a fucking fascist died.’ Everyone cheered.”44

This depiction of the events in Portland throughout 2020 is far from a peaceful representation of Antifa, and highlights the hatred and division that is overtaking the country. However, the author takes care to point out that the face of Antifa is white; “Many of the white protestors in Portland subscribe to a radical political agenda.”45 This distinction is likely a way to protect the validity of the Black Lives Matter movement and to counter the conservative assumption that those fighting for racial justice are violent; on the contrary, the article suggests that it is mostly white people who are responsible for non-peaceful acts of protest. Doubling back, the author highlights a statistic from the Center for Strategic and International Studies; “during the Trump administration, right-wing terrorists have carried out about a hundred and forty attacks, left-wing terrorists a dozen.”46 While careful to highlight the shortcomings and potential violence of Antifa, this statistic speaks for itself in shifting the blame to the right.

Public Opinion and Congressional Support

Unfortunately, there has been little research and reliable polling regarding public opinion on Antifa. In 2017, the website ISideWith published a poll asking respondents, “Should AntiFa be declared a domestic terrorist organization?” 64% of respondents said yes, while 36% said no.47 There was no available party breakdown.

A survey conducted in June of 2020 by Rasmussen Reports says that 49% of respondents believe that Antifa should be labeled as a terrorist organization, with 30% who disagree and 22% who are undecided; when broken down by political party, however, we see much more division, with 72% of Republicans in support of declaring Antifa a terrorist organization, compared to only 32% of Democrats and 43% of those not affiliated with either party.48

This makes perfect sense, of course- undoubtedly, there is more coverage of Antifa and their actions from conservative media than there is in liberal media. And even when liberal media discusses Antifa, they don’t portray them as a high-level, centralized, and powerful organization capable of taking down the entire government; they are careful to highlight the lack of centralized leadership, the other types of activism they do, and their goal of ending facism.

In Congress, a multitude of bills have been introduced in both the House and Senate (H.Res.525, 116th Congress; S.Res 279, 116th Congress; H.R.4117, 117th Congress, to name a few) put forth by Republican members of Congress attempting to label Antifa as a terrorist organization. None have passed. Many politicians, most Republican with a notable few Democrats, have denounced Antifa and their violent tactics. Not much information is available on the views of figures like President Biden, Senator Bernie Sanders, and Representative Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez on Antifa, other than describing Antifa as an ideology rather than a group, or condemning certain violent actions. While this might seem counterintuitive, it falls perfectly in line with the liberal media’s (scarce) portrayal of Antifa; by not paying the same attention to Antifa that conservatives do, they indicate to their audience that Antifa is not the threat that people may think is, but rather an ideological movement with loosely tied groups with no central authority. In this way, the Democratic party and liberal media distance themselves from any ties to Antifa and their actions.

Conclusion

The Republcian and Democratic parties are seemingly at war with one another. America is a political dystopia, with liberals and conservatives battling over a torn flag, any semblance of sanity and reason ripped from our citizens. Civil discourse is no longer civil, compromise is no longer calm, reason is no longer a reality. This lack of civility and understanding has left many Americans feeling trapped. Some feel they are trapped in the middle and are stuck watching the two parties shed blood to prove a political point, while others feel they are trapped in an extreme, fearing that the Nation will collapse if their team loses. There is a lot at stake here.

As with any high-stakes situation, people are willing to do just about anything for what they believe in. Naturally, this has led to an opportunity for extremist groups to dominate the media and gather national attention.

While neither QAnon nor Antifa represent the established, official political platforms of either party, they do represent the extremities of conservative and liberal ideology, respectively. Each of these groups has become the scapegoat of America’s problems by the opposing side, depicted as the downfall of American political society. While each side spends time attempting to discredit one another via these extremist groups, what do the parties themselves have to say about these claims?

We attempted to analyse the Republican/conservative response to QAnon and the Democratic/liberal response to Antifa. There were common themes: Republicans and Democrats alike attempted to dismiss any connection their party or ideology may have with the groups, as well as downplaying the propaganda surrounding the group by making them seem less damaging; for example, Tucker Carlson denied QAnon being a real threat in the weeks leading up to the insurrection on January 6th, similar to the ways liberal media jumped at the opportunity to portray Antifa as a decentralized, disorganized, activist organization- not a threat to our democracy. Additionally, liberals and conservatives alike were eager to point the finger back at their opponent, not only denying responsibility, but deflecting blame- we see this in Tucker Carlson’s claim that the left actually invented QAnon, and in the New Yorker article which described Antifa as a response to the right-wing. By avoiding the faults of their own party and actually utilizing those faults to shift blame to the opposition, the parties are successfully gatekeeping the media landscape. They are ensuring that their viewers only listen to the negatives of the other side, without having to confront their own parties’ faults. Simply put, they’re playing a game. And they will do everything in their power to win, without making it appear that they’re cheating.

The issue is that, if these liberal and conservative news networks and politicians do not denounce the violence and extremism, those members of the party who may already be polarized may see little to nothing wrong with joining these groups, and what’s more, engaging in violence. Seeing as the public is polarizing, if enough people engage with this violent mindset, it could have drastic consequences for our Nation.

This cycle can be broken, however. This type of hyper-partisan media only serves to polarize those who were already extreme to begin with.49 Perhaps if Americans were introduced to a relatively neutral, unbiased, and accountable news station, there would be little to no concern. There is a catch, however- people would have to trust that it is neutral or unbiased, and not only that, but it would have to be as popular and widespread as Fox, CNN, and MSNBC: a household name.

We would like to close with this glimmer of hope: changes may seem impossible if they are not initiated by politicians, mass media, or the government. Yet what is a Nation but a body of people? Think of the changes we can make in our daily lives to change the world in small but significant ways. If you truly care about what you believe in, you will do your best to engage with, listen to, and understand the other side. That sounds extremely difficult, maybe impossible, I know- but I think of it like this. A better world only exists with enough people that believe in making it better. When we work together, we can accomplish anything. Ultimately, most of us have a common goal: a better world. If we learn how to talk to one another, how to understand one another, we can show each other the worlds we’ve been dreaming of and make them a reality.
