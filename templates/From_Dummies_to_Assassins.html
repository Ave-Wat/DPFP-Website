From Dummies to Assassins: How Drones Became A Centerpiece of US Foreign Policy

By Rya Jetha and Hannah Jacobs

Drone (verb) make a continuous low humming sound

Drone (noun) a male bee in a colony of social bees, which does no work but can fertilize a queen

Before drones became an indispensable tool of counterterrorism, the word meant two things: insect and sound. The word took on a new meaning during World War II, when American soldiers training to shoot down planes named their dummies “target drones.”1 The phrase referred to the low humming sound made by the small planes and the disposable male bee that does not gather nectar or pollen, only lives to fertilize the queen, and is eventually killed off by his fellow bees. The name was apt for the American target drones – expendable dummies, fated to be shot down.

It would be many decades before drones evolved from serving as practice targets to targeting an enemy. Ancestors of the modern-day drone, such as aerial torpedoes, were used during the World Wars; however, the planes that launched these torpedoes were single-use projectiles while the contemporary drone is a reusable projectile-carrying machine. The real drone revolution came about around the turn of the century. In 1995, American technology and defence company General Atomics invented a remote-controlled spy plane that they called the Predator. It was first deployed in Kosovo during the NATO intervention to collect footage and illuminate targets with lasers. The Predator wasn’t killing targets yet; however, its name foreshadowed what was yet to come.

After NATO intervened in the Baltics, the U.S. government continued experimenting with drone technology. In fact, in September 2000, during an experimental flight over Kandahar, the epicenter of Taliban power in Afghanistan, one Predator drone unexpectedly found Osama Bin Laden standing in white robes near his compound.2 Though Bin Laden was a notorious terrorist leader at the time, known for coordinating twin suicide bombings at the Kenyan and Tanzanian U.S. embassies in 1998, the Predator that found him was not equipped with any weapons, and the U.S. was still undecided about its authority to kill him.3 Bin Laden got away, and al-Qaeda flew planes into the World Trade Center one year later. Shortly after Bin Laden escaped, the Predator was successfully tested firing an antitank missile. The CIA had transformed the Predator from a

After 9/11, the U.S. decided against a full-scale invasion of Afghanistan, choosing instead to pursue Operation Enduring Freedom, which involved the CIA, Special Forces, and the U.S. Air Force. The mission was to cleanse Afghanistan of al-Qaeda and their Taliban hosts. The first strike by a drone was conducted in October 2001 against a high profile target—Taliban Supreme Commander Mullah Mohammed Omar.5 The strike missed him. Instead, it killed his bodyguards, who were sitting in a car outside his compound. The first drone strike ever conducted sent a resounding message: the aerial assassins were imprecise, unreliable, and not yet ready to partake in a high-stakes invasion 7,000 miles away from the U.S. In fact, five days after the failed attempt at killing the Taliban Commander, a CIA team was almost bombed by an agency drone after the operating team mistook their own employees for al-Qaeda operatives.6 The CIA, however, was undeterred, seeing massive potential in their unmanned assassins. By December, even President Bush publicly advocated their use, telling his audience at The Military College of South Carolina that a secret CIA weapon was “able to circle over enemy forces, gather intelligence, transmit information instantly back to commanders, then fire on targets with extreme accuracy.” He proclaimed that “The military does not have enough unmanned vehicles,” thus kicking off a new era of warfare and counterterrorism.7

Over the twenty years that the U.S. has intervened in Afghanistan, drones have evolved from a scarcely tested, little-known device into a central feature of American foreign policy. They offer a transcendence of traditional military limitations during combat and in collecting intelligence, giving actors with drone technology an apparent eye of God over the enemy. Grégoire Chamayou, a political philosopher at Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique in Paris and author of A Theory of the Drone, writes that Jeremy Bentham’s panopticon has been reinvented for warfare through “winged and armed panoptics” that persistently surveille and terrorize the enemy.8 Unlike a soldier, the drone is free from the constraints of the human body, assuring governments a “constant geo-spatial ‘overwatch’” by the institutional eye9 and a “hunting ground” instead of a battlefield.10 Most importantly, drones promise a bloodless conflict for everyone but the enemy, making the elusive dream of “project[ing] power without projecting vulnerability” seem ever more attainable.11

In this piece, we seek to understand how the U.S. drone program has become a central feature of U.S. foreign policy by tracing its development through the lens of the U.S. intervention in Afghanistan. The first drone strike took place in Afghanistan in 2001, and now, as U.S. troops withdraw from Afghanistan, Afghanistan continues to command center stage. Before we tackle the implications of the U.S. withdrawal from Afghanistan for drone policy, we will go back in time to trace the evolution of the U.S. drone program in Afghanistan through the Bush, Obama, and Trump presidencies. We will explore how each president has approached the drone program, changed policies, and influenced debates around the topic.

Nodes, Kingpins, and Mowing the Grass: Counterrorism Strategy Before Drones

Presidential candidate George W. Bush was already thinking about drones in 1999. When speaking about his defence program at the Citadel Military school in South Carolina, he said “Our forces in the next century must be agile, lethal, readily deployed…. [Our] military must be

able to identify targets by a variety of means, then be able to destroy those targets almost instantly. We must be able to strike from across the world with pinpoint accuracy… with unmanned systems.”12 He continued to say that “Power is increasingly defined, not by mass or size, but by mobility and swiftness. Influence is measured in information, safety is gained in stealth, and force is projected on the long arc of precision-guided weapons.”13 His words foreshadowed a revolution in military technology that was well underway and a mode of American military thinking that goes back to the Vietnam War. However, unlike General William Westmoreland, who in 1969 predicted that the battlefield would have automated surveillance devices locating, tracking and targeting the enemy,14 soon-to-be President Bush was standing at the precipice of circumstances and technological advances that even he did not fully understand.

Six days after 9/11, President Bush signed a secret “Memorandum of Notification” giving the CIA authority to hunt down high-value targets within al-Qaeda.15 At the same time, President Bush did not waive the executive order banning assassinations.16 Instead, the Bush administration defined al-Qaeda operatives as “enemy combatants” in order to place them outside the purview of a legal ban against assassinations that had been crafted by military lawyer W. Hays Parks in 1989.17 President Bush even drew himself a pyramid showing enemies of importance, with Bin Laden right at the top.18 This pyramid represented a way of thinking about

The “critical node” strategy originated during World War II, when the U.S. Air Force theorized that finding and destroying critical nodes in the war effort, such as railways and oil refineries, would cause a collapse in the enemy war effort and an end to the war.19 However, none of the destruction brought about enemy collapse. Collecting intelligence about critical nodes proved difficult, leading to a widening of possible targets. In the end, everything became a possible target, rendering the strategy indiscriminate and ineffective. This same theory was applied decades later to the war in Vietnam, with U.S. generals asserting that destroying the critical node in the war — the route from the North to the South — would bring an end to the war. Once again, identifying critical nodes proved impossible.20

From the inception of the critical node strategy, the target nodes had been things, not people. This changed in 1991 when Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait. The Pentagon temporarily identified him as the most important node to target in order to collapse the invasion effort until an employee remembered that assassination was banned by the U.S. government.21 Although Saddam Hussain’s name did not stay on an assassination list for long, a domestic policy was developing at the same time in the U.S. that classified people as targets. Named the “kingpin strategy” and spearheaded by the Drug Enforcement Administration in the early 1990s, the theory worked off the same assumption as the critical node theory: the U.S. faced a hierarchical threat that could be destroyed by targeting its fundamental components.22 The DEA began targeting the “kingpins'' and henchmen of drug cartels in the U.S. and Latin America with the goal of rendering them ineffective by destroying their command and control structures. Although this strategy proved highly ineffective — drug prices actually decreased when kingpins were killed — after 9/11 the strategy was transplanted from the U.S. and Latin America to the Middle East, where it informed President Bush’s pyramid of critical or kingpin targets and was applied to al-Qaeda. Additionally, the close relationship between the U.S. and Israeli governments meant that the Israel intelligence agency’s strategy of “mowing the grass” — continuously killing terrorists in a network until only inexperienced and ineffective actors are left behind — was incorporated into U.S. counterterrorism thinking.23 According to the theory, if one “mowed the grass” for long enough using “targeted killings,” the “barrel of terror” would be drained. Just weeks after 9/11, the U.S. used this strategy in its failed attempt to kill critical node Commander Mullah Mohammed Omar, signalling that Afghanistan was now the testing ground of American counterterrorism strategy.

These changes made it easier to confuse a van carrying targets with a van carrying innocent people, or a group of men exercising with an al-Qaeda training camp. The number of drone strikes skyrocketed, civilian death tolls appeared to plummet, and drone strikes received less negative press. These shifts in thinking about drone targets would have profound impacts on U.S. drone policy as President Barack Obama entered office in 2009, pledging to end the ‘forever wars’ in the Middle East.

In a speech at the University of Chicago in 2016, Obama conceded that the drone program had little legal standing. Title 50 authority provided leeway for the CIA to conduct drone strikes while keeping them secret.42 An action declared under Title 50 is classified as covert and is described as “an activity or activities of the United States Government to influence political, economic, or military conditions abroad, where it is intended that the role of the United States will not be apparent or acknowledged publicly.”43 Essentially, Title 50 allows little disclosure of drone strikes, removing requirements for transparency and accountability. To at least partially remove the shroud of secrecy around drones and encourage more public debate about drone use, Obama tried to bring the program to light by giving the U.S. Air Force more control over the drone program instead of the CIA.44

The Quagmire Continues

Before Donald Trump assumed the Presidency in 2017, he was adamant about his views when it came to the forever wars: he wanted them to end. During his campaign, he insisted that the U.S. would stay out of these conflicts, but like his predecessors, he too fell into the “quagmire of war.”48 By the summer of 2017, Trump had already increased the number of troops in the Middle East, including Afghanistan, by a total of 33%.49 Trump authorized more ground troops, an additional 4,000 to the existing 11,000.50 Accompanying these additional boots on the ground, the U.S. military also “dropped twice as many bombs on the Taliban and the newly formed branch of ISIS in Afghanistan than it did in all of 2016.”51 According to the Bureau of Investigative Journalism, Trump oversaw 2,243 drone strikes in the first two years of his presidency, while Obama authorized 1,878 during his eight years in office.”52 Trump escalated the war in Afghanistan, even though he said that he had planned to completely withdraw American troops.

This drone escalation was possible in part because President Trump eased restrictions that the Obama administration had applied to the drone program. Journalist Umar A. Farooq writes: “In Trump's first year of office, his administration eased two restrictions for drone strikes: removing the limitation that only allowed strikes on high-ranking militants and removing the previous vetting process for drone strikes. The U.S. also relaxed a restriction on its rules of engagement so that it no longer had to be in direct contact with enemy forces to conduct airstrikes.”53 Essentially, Trump relaxed the process for identifying whether someone posed a threat to the U.S. Rather than requiring that a terrorist target pose a “continuing and imminent threat,” as was the requirement established by the Obama administration, lower-level enemies, such as foot soldiers, could be targeted by drones. The Future of Drone Policy

Counterterrorism drone warfare reached its fourth American president when Joe Biden was sworn in this year. However, how Biden will influence U.S. drone policy is uncertain. In March 2021, the New York Times reported that Biden had begun reviewing the legal and policy frameworks that regulate drone targetings. “This review includes an examination of previous approaches in the context of evolving counterterrorism threats in order to refine our approach going forward,” Emily Horne, a spokeswoman for the National Security Council said. “In addition, the review will seek to ensure appropriate transparency measures.”55 Reports also state that the Biden administration is having conversations about whether to return to the Obama-era approach, which had more centralized oversight and higher levels of intelligence collection, or to continue with Trump-era policies, which were decentralized with fewer accountability measures.56 It should be noted that Biden’s Director of National Intelligence is Avril Haines, who served as Deputy National Security Advisor and Deputy Director of the CIA in the Obama administration. Haines is regarded as a key architect of Obama’s drone program expansion, and a 2013 Newsweek profile of her claims that she “was sometimes summoned in the middle of the night to weigh in on whether a suspected terrorist could be lawfully incinerated by a drone strike.”57 Whether Haines’ role in the new administration will have any bearing on the direction of Biden’s drone policy is yet to be seen, especially in the face of growing public pressure to end the drone wars. Biden himself alluded to the future of the U.S. drone program in Afghanistan at a White House briefing on July 8th, when he announced that the U.S. is “developing a counterterrorism over-the-horizon capability that will allow us to keep our eyes firmly fixed on any direct threats to the United States in the region [Afghanistan], and act quickly and decisively if needed.”59 CNN reported on July 3rd that the U.S. military will retain authority to carry out strikes against the Taliban in support of Afghan forces.60 Conversations in the Biden administration at the moment are focused on giving immediate humanitarian assistance to Afghans by keeping the airport open in Kabul, and not on a long-term counterterrorism strategy that could involve drones.61 Whether the Biden administration adopts drone strategies in the long term that rely on the critical node theory, kingpin strategy, or mow-the-grass approach, or if drone policy departs from historically accepted ideas about counterterrorism is uncertain. What is clear in the present is that Afghanistan’s fall to the Taliban is a major turning point for U.S. foreign policy and will likely dictate key components of the Biden drone policy going forward.
