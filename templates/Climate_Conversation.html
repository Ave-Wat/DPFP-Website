Collectivism v. Individualism in the World of Climate Change

Jared Garson and Ellen Wilhelm

The following is a refined conversation between a liberal and conservative about the topic of climate change and how it should be addressed. The liberal on the left is in blue and the conservative on the right is in red.

The US has procrastinated climate action for long enough. We’ve known since before the 21st century that the increasing global temperature is on track to alter our very way of life if we don’t address it sooner rather than later. Now that it is later, everything we should have been doing over the last twenty years needs to be compressed into the next ten. The first step is to heavily invest in renewable energy so we can reach net-zero emissions as soon as possible. The next step is to shift technology from carbon-powered to electricity-powered, so renewable energy can power parts of the market that it couldn't before. To get here, we’ll need to apply a carbon tax to discourage carbon emissions, encourage carbon-neutral business models, and fund investment in sustainable technology.

In an analysis of climate change as a social issue, Frantz and Mayer conclude that successful action demands continued collective action, and collective action demands convenience. Many people will not take individual action unless it is easy and directly in front of them. Instead of fear-mongering, it’s important to provide solutions by pushing for climate-neutral public transportation instead of the widening of major highways, for example. Roser-Renouf et al. conducted a study to distinguish the key beliefs that lead to political action. They conclude that “legislation is unlikely without … citizen activism.” Big governments will not enact carbon limits without public pressure and there will be no public pressure if we cannot come together to demand collective action as a country. In a study assessing collectivist and individualist cultures, Kiang et al. found that if the government is unsupportive of climate-friendly behavior, companies will not invest in climate-friendly opportunities, which will “foster consumers’ climate change inaction.”

There is still significant uncertainty in the scientific community as to whether your timeline is accurate. To a certain extent, Democrats have exaggerated Climate Change’s urgency. I agree that Climate Change exists and that it is an issue, but I don’t think we’ll reach a ‘point of no return’ where humanity’s course in history becomes irreversible.

Frantz and Mayer reference Latane and Darley’s model of helping behavior in an emergency and they tie it to climate change inaction. They argue that five criteria must be met in order for a person to help. They must notice the event in question, interpret the event as an emergency, feel personally responsible, know what to do to help, and finally, make the decision to act. A person can notice an event but fail to act because they do not see it as an emergency. That is exactly what is happening with many Americans.

There’s a consistent scientific consensus that the global temperature is on track to increase by a destructive amount of degrees. I don’t know if you can claim that the scientific community’s timeline is inaccurate.

Whether the planet heats up by 1.5 degrees Celsius or 3 degrees Celsius over the next 100 years, there are many strategies like sea walls that humans can use to adapt until we get to the point where moving away from fossil fuels is economically feasible. As of right now, if we implemented many of the policies that Democrats tend to champion, the working class and especially the lower class would be hit the hardest.

Again, we see some people struggle with the notion of recognizing climate change as an emergency. Frantz and Mayer note several reasons as to why a person may fail to assess a situation as an emergency: it can be difficult to notice a degree or two Celsius change, especially since climate changes are not equal across the globe. Some regions are hit harder than others, and the effects of climate change can manifest differently. People also spend far more time indoors in climate-controlled environments than in generations past. Less time outdoors equates to being less deeply rooted in their local environment, and thus less likely to notice the subtle changes like slight temperature increases.

They’ll be hit the hardest if we can’t properly mitigate Climate Change. Low-income communities are the most vulnerable to forest fires, droughts, flooding, and job insecurity, all of which will continue to increase at a frightening rate. But that’s beside the point because investing in renewable energy won’t hurt the economy; if anything, it’ll make it stronger! Investing in green technology would create many more jobs than it would erase, not to mention it would do the economy a favor by phasing out an outdated and harmful technology with a more sustainable alternative.

Investing in renewable energy isn’t the problem. The problem is when the government hikes up the cost of energy with taxes or by ending fossil fuel subsidies. Many states heavily rely on the oil industry to keep their communities afloat, and their economy would crumble if it was taken away. You mentioned a carbon tax earlier, which is essentially a tax that companies pay for emitting carbon. A carbon tax would cause two things to happen: first, many companies that the country depends on for jobs and revenue would relocate to a different country without a carbon tax. It’s just the smart financial move to make; second, the companies that remain in the US would just pass these costs onto the consumer by charging higher prices for gas, heating, and electricity. Low-income households tend to be the most reliant on carbon technology and the least capable of transitioning to newer, expensive renewable technology. The truth is that a carbon tax would hurt the working and lower class, and would stifle economic growth too.

Corporations aren’t excused to pollute and exploit the environment just because they’re in the US. They need to be held accountable for their harmful business practices. And if these businesses do pass some of the carbon tax on to the consumer and the cost of energy goes up for the working and lower class, there are ways around that. For instance, the carbon tax could fund a monthly check that would go to the most impacted Americans.

Sweden’s carbon tax was one of the first to be implemented, and it has been a success. They have simultaneously reduced carbon emissions and maintained GDP growth. In 2019, the carbon tax raised 2.3 billion USD. From its implementation in 1990 until 2018, Sweden’s carbon emissions decreased by 27%. On its own, it might not sound like much, but imagine the global impact that could have if every country followed suit. Sweden isn’t the only country to successfully implement carbon taxes either; so have Finland and Canada.

The point I’m trying to make is that the market will naturally adjust to a more sustainable technology over time, so enforcing a carbon tax would just stunt the economy for no good reason. It’s important to strike a balance between moving away from fossil fuels and also protecting the health of the economy, both of which can happen as the market naturally adjusts.

If we had all the time in the world, we would eventually move away from fossil fuels and embrace more sustainable forms of energy without government intervention. The problem is that we don’t have all the time in the world. Whether we have 10 years or 20 years to address this issue, if we don’t make a significant effort to combat climate change by then, many, many people, not just in the US but around the world, will suffer from even more severe weather, rising oceans, and frequent forest fires. The market is not on track to naturally adopt renewable energy in time to avoid these disasters, so it needs a push from the government to get there.

As previously mentioned, a person must interpret an event as an emergency before acting. Here we have an example of what it looks like to not only interpret the climate change issue as an emergency, but also feel personally responsible, know what to do to help, and finally make the decision to act. This is citizen activism at work.

Let's say that hypothetically the US does become carbon neutral by 2050. We aren’t even the biggest carbon emitters in the world. What’s the point of spending all this money eliminating fossil fuels if China will just continue polluting and polluting? Nearly none of the countries that signed the Paris Climate Agreement are on track to meet their goal. If the US somehow manages to go carbon neutral, it won’t even put a dent in the problem you’re talking about.

The US is responsible for emitting more greenhouse gases than any other country in the world. Currently, China’s overtaken us, but we’ve still had an enormous output over the years. Once the US develops better renewable technology, the country can regain its place as a global leader in the world. The US could invest its technology in other developing countries, saving them resources, gaining influence, and putting pressure on other countries as well. It seems like a win-win situation.

The United States’ per capita greenhouse gas emissions for 2017 was about 3.5 tons of CO2 per person higher than Russia, and far outranking Japan, EU-28, China, Brazil, and India. In 2019, The Carbon Brief also cited the US as having the largest CO2 emissions since 1750. Carbon emissions are a cumulative problem, but as a global leader and one of the worst carbon emissions offenders, it is of critical importance that the US set a climate-friendly precedent for the rest of the world to follow.

Fortunately, I’m on board with investing in renewable energy, but not proposals like a carbon tax which would harm the American worker. As of right now, the biggest hindrance to renewable energy is that when the sun isn’t shining or the wind isn’t blowing, it’s harder to generate electricity. That’s why we can both agree that the US should invest in sustainable alternatives like nuclear energy while building better batteries that can hold massive stores of energy.

In the study conducted by Xiang et al., they concluded that there are three psychological barriers associated with fewer climate-ameliorative food choice intentions: denial, conflicting goals and aspirations, and tokenism. The above argument is a prime example of conflicting goals and aspirations. The conservative may agree that as a society, we should be investing in sustainable energy, but they also may not agree that instituting a carbon tax is the way to accomplish the goal. Thus from their perspective, the goal of sustainability and the aspiration of a carbon tax cannot coexist.

I think any money we can invest in sustainable energy would be money well spent. I’m glad to hear that we’re on the same page with this one. Although you don’t share my sense of urgency and I’m more intent on carbon taxes than you are, I’m glad that we have a starting point. Once we begin investing more in renewable energy, we can decide on the next step.
