Religion and the Vaccine: Why Americans of diverse religious backgrounds have rejected—or accepted—the coronavirus vaccine, and what it means for our future

By Michaela Skelley and Isabel Pastoor

As Americans adjust to a world with covid-19, solutions for fighting the pandemic are ever changing. As students return to college and employers to their jobs, proof of vaccination is coming with them. However, as vaccination requirements continue to be established across America, more people have begun to reject the covid-19 vaccine on what they claim to be religious grounds. These religious claims come with many consequences, not for only those refusing the vaccine but for all Americans. The aim of this article is to objectively examine faith driven attitudes towards the vaccine within the Islamic, Jewish, and Christian faiths. Then, we aim to critically analyze the potential effects and consequences of religiously motivated rejection of the vaccine.

Islamic Faith

From India to Indonesia, Muslims around the world voiced concerns about the possible presence of porcine products in the coronavirus vaccine at least since late 2020. Chemically purified gelatin derived from pigs is found in many drugs and vaccines, making them “haram,” or forbidden, to Muslims. Vaccines not containing pork gelatin have shorter shelf lives and are more expensive to make, so pork-free alternatives to most vaccines are not likely to be widely available any time soon.

But Pfizer, Moderna, and AstraZeneca have all confirmed that no porcine products are in their covid vaccines. Those three vaccines have since been widely ruled “halal,” or legally permissible, by many Islamic bodies and promoted by imams and other community leaders across the world, including in the U.S. And mosques across the country are giving out vaccines.

In Santa Ana, Muslim activist Rida Hamida brought a mobile vaccination clinic to the local Islamic Center, along with a food truck serving halal tacos, for a hybrid, cross-cultural event combining socializing with vaccine promotion. In Chicago, the Mosque Foundation held a Pfizer vaccination drive. And during the holy month of Ramadan, Imam Asad Zaman, executive director of the Muslim American Society of Minnesota, led a vaccine campaign across 16 mosques in the state.

Imam Mohamed Magid, former president of the Islamic Society of North America, asserts that the coronavirus vaccine is halal and that vaccination is an honorable act in Islam. In an interview with Interfaith Youth Core, he advised Muslims that “by taking the vaccine you actually get rewarded by God Almighty for preventing harm to others. Remember, there's a verse in the Holy Quran as well as in the Old Testament: If you save one life, it is as if you have saved the life of all humanity. You're doing the most honorable act of worship — the most sacred act of worship is protecting life.”

On the history of Islamic thought about vaccination, Imam Magid added, “Muslims are among the first people to believe in the idea of vaccination…The idea of preventing harm comes from the Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him, who said, if there's any contagious disease in a city, you should not enter that city or leave it. If you contract that sickness, you should not go on to spread it. This is the theological foundation for vaccination.”

Another cause for concern over the vaccine came during Ramadan, when some Muslims worried that taking the vaccine would break their religiously mandated fast. During Ramadan, Muslims are forbidden from ingesting anything during the day, including water and oral medication. But Muslim scholars and public health officials have publicly declared that getting vaccinated during Ramadan is permitted.

It is difficult to know the extent to which concerns about porcine products in the coronavirus vaccine or its acceptability during Ramadan have dissuaded Muslims from getting vaccinated. The Muslim American population is small, and studies that report vaccine attitudes and behaviors by religious affiliation do not include or disaggregate Muslims. Even in communities with large Muslim populations, such as the Twin Cities in Minnesota, quantitative data on covid 19 vaccination rates and attitudes among Muslims is nonexistent.

But with numerous Islamic legal rulings permitting the covid vaccine, and persistent community led efforts to vaccinate Muslim populations underway, it is reasonable to expect Islamic religiously-rooted objections to the vaccine to wither.

Jewish Faith

Orthodox Jewish communities, especially those in New York, have come under fire from media and local governments for rejecting the coronavirus vaccination. This rejection comes after some Orthodox populations were hit hard by the pandemic, in part because they did not follow social distancing guidelines or restrictions on religious and other gatherings. Funerals, weddings, worship, and other gatherings proceeded as normal, resulting in covid-19 test positivity rates far above those in surrounding areas. Community defiance of public health guidelines for the sake of practicing religion brought intense attention to these communities and controversy, along with several in-depth journalistic investigations of the issue.

The release of the covid-19 vaccine in the U.S. has brought renewed attention to these small communities, as some use religious reasoning to denounce and reject the vaccine. Dr. Bat-Sheva Lerner Maslow, a Jewish reproductive endocrinologist, worries that “fears about the vaccine and fertility have caught on in the Orthodox community like wildfire.” Some Orthodox women, who see bearing children as an important religious obligation, fear that the vaccine causes infertility.

Other Orthodox Jews believe it is against Jewish law to take an “experimental” vaccine.

While numerous rabbis and other Orthodox leaders are encouraging their community members to get vaccinated, a few vocal opponents of the vaccine have brought negative attention to Orthodox Jews. Camp Hikon, an Orthodox summer camp in New York, was the subject of controversy when it explicitly banned vaccinated campers and staff. The camp’s website claims that sleeping near vaccinated people can cause the virus to spread.

Anecdotal reports of extreme anti-vaccination attitudes among Orthodox Jewish communities are somewhat at odds with empirical data on Jews’ attitudes toward the covid-19 vaccine. “The idea that Orthodox Jews don’t vaccinate is just patently false,” Dr. Miriam Knoll, a Jewish radiation oncologist, said in an interview with the New York Times. “We absolutely do, and there are countless Orthodox rabbis who would say it is a religious obligation to vaccinate.”

A March 2021 survey of 5,149 American adults conducted by the Public Religion Research Institute found that Jews were the most likely (85%) of all religious groups surveyed to fall into the “vaccine accepters” category, which includes those who have received the vaccine or state that they will receive the vaccine as soon as possible.

On other measures, Jews stand out, too. They are less likely to have major concerns about the vaccine and more likely to have very few or no concerns compared to all other religious groups. Half of Jews surveyed support the individual right to refuse vaccination.

When it comes to religious beliefs about the vaccine, Jews are most likely (69%) to agree with the statement that getting vaccinated against covid-19 is a way to live out the religious principle of loving their neighbors and least likely (12%) to believe that God will protect the faithful from covid-19. And Jews were the least likely (18%) of all religious groups to say that they would look to a religious leader for information about getting vaccinated. This could mean that Jews who are vaccine-hesitant would not be swayed by religious leaders who attempt to persuade their communities to get vaccinated. It is important to note, however, that Jewish respondents to the Public Religion Research Institute’s survey were not exclusively Orthodox Jews, and that vaccine hesitancy might be disproportionately high in Orthodox communities.

In Judaism, Pikuach Nefesh, the obligation to save a life or a soul, is of high importance. Jewish doctrine requires Jews to prioritize life and saving lives if faced with a moral dilemma. Pikuach Nefesh is so important that Jews are permitted to break other holy laws to honor it. Getting vaccinated against the coronavirus can be seen as a way for Jews to live out this principle. The Torah in Leviticus 19:16 states, “Do not stand idly by the blood of your neighbor.”

The Orthodox Union's official statement regarding the covid-19 vaccine states, “The Torah obligation to preserve our lives and the lives of others requires us to vaccinate for Covid-19 as soon as a vaccine becomes available.”

Christian Faiths

When examining the validity for refusing the covid vaccine on religious grounds in the Islamic and Jewish faiths, there seems to be somewhat concrete instruction on vaccination. However, unlike for many Muslims and Jews, there is no concept of kosher or halal that prevents or allows Christians to take part in a certain practice. Most Christians rely primarily on the word of God for guidance on what decisions to make, while religious leaders take second priority. Christianity also includes different branches that have different traditions of interpreting scripture. This allows for a great diversity of opinions regarding the covid-19 vaccine.

Catholicism

Catholic resistance to the vaccine has been largely driven by the belief that the vaccine was produced using aborted fetal cells. There is some validity to this concern; some vaccine testing uses cell lines originally developed from fetal cells obtained from abortions in the 70s and 80s. Though just two abortions from decades ago provided the cells necessary to test and develop several vaccines and save millions of lives, this slight association of the covid-19 vaccine with abortion made many Catholics hesitant to take it. However, similar to Muslims’ concerns about pork gelatin in the vaccine, Catholics’ concerns about aborted fetal cells being used in vaccine development turned out to be unnecessary. Neither the Pfizer nor Moderna vaccines were developed or tested using fetal cell lines.

Catholics around the world rely heavily on the Pope and the Vatican for guidance on moral issues, while in Protestant Christianity there is no single divine leader who is tasked with relaying the word of God to followers. This offers Protestants more room for interpretation when considering advice from their religious leaders. But for Catholics, the Pope has the final word. The Vatican’s doctrinal office, the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith, has determined that it is “morally acceptable” for Catholics to get the covid-19 vaccine. The Vatican has asserted that Catholics who reject the coronavirus vaccine "must do their utmost to avoid… becoming vehicles for the transmission of the infectious agent.”

Catholics have surveyed and polled as more accepting of the coronavirus vaccine than other Christians. 64 percent were accepting of it in a Public Religion Research Institute survey, and just eight percent said that they would refuse it outright. Catholics are also more likely (56%) than all Protestants (51%) and Evangelicals (46%) to believe that getting vaccinated against covid-19 is a way to live out the religious principle of loving their neighbors. But these relatively high acceptance rates compared to other Christians might not be a result of the Pope’s advice. Only 31 percent of Catholics said that they would or did look to a religious leader for advice on the covid-19 vaccine.

Evangelical Protestantism

Evangelical Protestants are perhaps the religious group most strongly criticized and scrutinized for their rejection of the coronavirus vaccine, a rejection that some Evangelical Protestants claim has religious grounds. In a survey conducted by the Public Religion Research Institute in March 2021, only 45 percent of white Evangelicals responded that they had either received the covid-19 vaccine or intended to. A Pew Research Center poll from February reported 54 percent of all Evangelicals either already had, definitely would, or probably would get the vaccine.

Evangelical Protestants’ rejection of the vaccine is strongly tied to their tendency to be more conservative, more supportive of conspiracy theories, and poorer, so their high rates of vaccination hesitancy and rejection are likely not determined by their religion alone. Their high rate of vaccine hesitancy is also a result of characteristics that are correlated with their religion, such as class, education, and political affiliation.

Among Republicans, white Evangelical Christians are more likely than other religious groups to believe in certain conspiracy theories, according to a study by the conservative American Enterprise Institute. According to the study’s author, Daniel Cox, "There is a tendency within white Christian nationalism to want to believe these kinds of conspiracies, because it reinforces the idea of an us versus them.” An example of a conspiracy that has spread among some Evangelical Christians during the pandemic is the belief that the covid-19 vaccine is the “mark of the beast.” Revelation 13:7 states that “No one can buy or sell unless he has the mark, that is, the name of the beast or the number of its name.” Some Christians have viewed restrictions preventing unvaccinated individuals from attending certain events or having access to universities as evidence that what they read in Revelation about the end times is unfolding now.

Some Evangelicals also believe that taking the vaccine diminishes one’s trust in God and makes medicine an idol, or a false god. Evangelicals are more likely than other Christians and Jews to believe that God will protect the faithful from covid-19. Philippians 2:4 states, “Let each of you look not only to his own interests, but also to the interests of others.” This scripture is consistently brought up when contemplating receiving the covid-19 vaccine.

The Bible does not speak directly about vaccines, but it does call on people to always fight for and preserve life. William Franklin Graham is an American evangelist and missionary and the president and CEO of the Billy Graham Evangelistic Association as well as Samaritan's Purse, an international Christian relief organization. The granddaughter of Rev. Franklin Graham, Jerusha Duford, recently said, “I want the church to fight for others more than they're fighting for themselves; that's what I have not seen over the last year. And that's what we're called to do." Despite many influential Evangelical leaders endorsing the vaccine (in a recent survey from the National Association of Evangelicals, 89 percent of Evangelical leaders said that they would encourage others to get vaccinated), some leaders have been just as vocally opposed to it.

The ability to individually interpret scripture and receive revelation could be behind the diversity of vaccine attitudes and behaviors among Christians. Unlike Judaism and Islam, Christianity does not dictate which substances and medications are allowed and prohibited for consumption. Scripture is up for interpretation. Some Christians may read the command to “love thy neighbor as thyself” (Matthew 36:39) as a command to get vaccinated for the sake of protecting their neighbors. However, most Evangelicals seem to believe the scripture means they should prevent others from getting a vaccine that they believe is unsafe. The denomination is the least likely (46 percent) of any Christian group to believe that getting vaccinated against the coronavirus reflects a religious love for their neighbors.

Implications

Just half of all Americans are fully vaccinated, and many of the unvaccinated do not plan to get the shot. Stalling vaccination rates, loosening of public health guidelines, increased domestic and international travel, and the new hyper-infectious Delta variant all point to a rising wave of covid-19. Cities, businesses, and universities across the U.S. are bringing back mask mandates, and workers are returning home to telecommute.

Any significant population that holds out on getting vaccinated against covid-19 can prolong the pandemic in the U.S. and delay health, social, and economic recovery. The size of unvaccinated populations, their travel and contact with others, and their adherence to other public health guidelines, such as masking and social distancing, will all determine the extent of the negative externalities of refusing the vaccine.

In an effort to increase vaccination rates and limit the harmful effects of the Delta variant, some authorities are taking restrictions a step further and mandating or strongly recommending vaccination as a requirement for access to public and private spaces and services. New York City will require proof of vaccination for covid-19 for those who want to dine in restaurants, use gyms, and attend performances. New York City Mayor Bill de Blasio said, “If you’re unvaccinated, unfortunately, you will not be able to participate in many things.” This strategy of making life hard for the unvaccinated may be more effective than attempting to address individual reasons for resistance to the vaccine. In the coming weeks and months, the country will see the extent to which unvaccinated individuals will be excluded from public life. Depending on where they live, unvaccinated individuals could experience difficulty finding and keeping jobs, enrolling their children in public schools, eating at restaurants, and attending public events.

California, North Carolina, and New York states have all required state employees to be vaccinated. Local governments can require their employees to be vaccinated, too, and some have. Essentially, most employers can require vaccination. Walmart, The Walt Disney Company, Google, Facebook, Tyson Foods, and Uber have all required some chunk of their employees to get the vaccine, and more companies could follow. Private colleges and universities can require faculty and staff to be vaccinated and demand proof of vaccination—a requirement that has proven more difficult for public schools to implement.

Schools requiring their students to get vaccinated is nothing new. Public and private schools alike commonly require students to get vaccinated against chickenpox, measles, mumps and rubella, diphtheria, tetanus and pertussis, and polio. New on the list this year for all those who are old enough to be inoculated is the covid-19 vaccine. While the majority of states (44 and Washington, D.C.) have legal provisions for religious exemptions from vaccination, Maine, New York, and Connecticut recently removed their provisions. More states could follow. In the states that don’t allow students to claim religious exemptions from the covid-19 vaccine, parents who refuse to have their children vaccinated on religious grounds could struggle to enroll them in school.

About 41 million white Evangelical adults live in the U.S., and fewer than half of them report that they intend to get vaccinated. If their attitudes don’t change, that means more than 20 million white Evangelicals will be unvaccinated. This population has also been vocally opposed to masking, social distancing, and following public health requirements during the pandemic. Not all of this resistance is religious; Evangelicals are largely conservative, making up an important voter base for Trump in both 2016 and 2020, and they oppose government regulation of health decisions. Their strong religious and political convictions make them a hard group to convince to get vaccinated. In areas with large Evangelical populations, recovery from covid-19 could be substantially delayed.

Implications for Religious Communities

Refusing the covid-19 vaccine on religious grounds will not only have significant public health consequences but could harm faith communities as well. This will be seen heavily within Evangelical communities that strongly defend their right to reject covid-19 vaccines on religious grounds. In an interview with David Carment, the teaching and evangelizing Pastor at Liberty Church, a non-denominational church in Jersey City, Pastor Carment stated that there are no biblical grounds for rejecting the covid-19 vaccine and “Anyone who says there are, is misconstruing scripture.” Pastor Carment explores why refusing the covid vaccine is so damaging when he says that “As Christians we are known for being pro-life by any means necessary, but what it can often boil down to is just abortions.” According to Pastor Carment if all Christians were pro-life, then they would be leading the charts for vaccination rates. When asked about his stance on the covid-19 vaccine, Pastor Carment asked, “Why would Christians not want to show themselves to be pro-life when they are presented with the opportunity to do so through vaccination?” Christians who see getting vaccinated as a moral imperative may feel frustrated with Christians who take an anti-vaccination stance, an internal division that could weaken the Church as a whole.

As vaccination requirements continue to be implemented, religious communities will try to use new restrictions as proof that they are being persecuted. This sense of alienation will continue to further the exclusive “us versus them” mentality discussed previously in this article, potentially turning more people away from the faith.

Churches whose leaders have publicly denounced the covid-19 vaccine or that simply have large numbers of unvaccinated members may alienate worshippers concerned about their health. Since many Evangelicals are against vaccination, many believers may not feel comfortable attending church in person due to high rates of unvaccinated individuals in these spaces. While limits on religious gatherings have been removed, not all members are returning.

In the Catholic Church, members are required to attend Sunday Mass “as the primordial day of holy obligation.” In 2020, when the pandemic struck, bishops excused worshippers from attending in person as part of an exemption that is called a “dispensation.” But now that dispensations are being lifted, Catholics have a religious requirement to attend Mass in person again. Catholics in these areas who are concerned about low vaccination rates may have limited options—find another church, risk their health to attend mass among the unvaccinated, or stop going altogether.

This demonstrates how some people’s refusal of the covid vaccine on religious grounds can impact the religious liberty of those who do not feel safe gathering with unvaccinated individuals. The availability of online worship options, where churches stream their services for viewers at home, allows people to avoid the risks of in-person gatherings while continuing to practice religion. Online worship options mean that there is less pressure to go back to the physical church space, potentially fracturing and disconnecting religious communities, as some members will never have the opportunity to interact.

Conclusion

The First Amendment of the U.S. Constitution provides Americans with distinct religious freedoms, religious freedoms that provide Americans with the choice of whether they will reject the covid-19 vaccine. While religious freedoms should always be protected, it is important to be able to distinguish between a religious right and a personal conviction. The need to understand how religion and acceptance of the covid-19 vaccines are connected is more important now than ever before. Religion and vaccine acceptance are not problems of separate spheres, but rather they coincide and affect the future of all Americans.
