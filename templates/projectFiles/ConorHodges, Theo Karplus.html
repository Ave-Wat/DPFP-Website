“Revolutionary Healthcare: How 1970’s Radicals Fought Addiction and Terrified Moderates”

By Conor Hodges and Theo Karplus

Not all views expressed in this paper are held by all of the authors.

From 1971 to 1978 heroin addicts at the South Bronx’s largest hospital received prescriptions for revolutionary action. Instead of returning to Lincoln Hospital every few days to pick up prescriptions of methadone to fight drug addictions, patients received acupuncture therapy and an eighty six page pamphlet titled “The Opium Trail: Heroin and Imperialism”. Readers learned that methadone treatment made them “dependent and subservient” on the Nixon Administration and that “The Best Form of Therapy” was to join the Black Panther Party and Young Lords in “a mass movement fighting against racism and poverty.”

By the early 1970’s and amid a panic over rising crime rates, state and federal officials linked addiction to crime and began replacing rehabilitation services with expanded policing and criminal sentencing. “Crime reduction,” a 1973 Department of Justice report observed, “remains the central objective of all methadone programs.” Methadone alleviated symptoms of heroin withdrawal without providing an opioid high, but it also required injections multiple times a week. Officials began using methadone maintenance therapy as an incentive, requiring participants to return to their clinics regularly in hopes of enrolling more in job-training and counseling programs. They hoped that attaching methadone therapy to employment and psychiatric assistance would reduce crime.

Black and Puerto Rican radicals argued that methadone maintenance simply replaced addiction to heroin with addiction to traditional ideologies; methadone-dependent patients could lose their medication if they ran afoul of the law by, for instance, joining the protests against police violence, racism and poverty. To the leftists in the Black Panther Party and Young Lords, healthcare policy was being used as a weapon to suppress their anti-capitalist and anti-racist movements.

Lincoln Hospital’s Detox program was the revolutionary response. Both rebel groups joined a 1970 healthcare worker strike at Lincoln which ultimately succeeded at seizing control of the hospital – and a one million dollar annual drug rehabilitation funding flow from New York State. The Young Lords took over day-to-day operation of the program with the intent of turning it into a form of healthcare that 1. Rejected methadone as slavery by a different name, 2. Encouraged patients to join the radical movement, and 3. Organized participants to fight against what leftists saw as the root causes of addiction: racism and poverty.

Healthcare providers and state officials didn’t see methadone therapy as a counterrevolutionary weapon. Instead, they earnestly wanted addiction treatments to decrease “antisocial behavior” such as crime and to increase “social productivity” such as employment. Definitions of what was antisocial and what was productive, however, were heavily racialized. Black anger with police violence and poverty were derided by White psychologists as “protest

psychosis,” resulting in treatments intended to blunt racial anxieties instead of treating the root cause.

On the other hand, acupuncture therapy seemed to show immense success in treating drug addictions, specifically heroin. Vicente “Panama” Alba, a former member of the Young Lords Party and counselor at Lincoln Detox Center, explained in a 2013 interview that leaders of the program implemented acupuncture after reading about its use in Thailand to treat opium addiction. At the heart of this treatment was the insight that “an individual’s addiction wasn’t just a physical problem, but a psychological problem.” Imperialism abroad and oppression at home had driven many into addiction, and so public health would be best served through treatment that integrated political education and therapeutic interventions.

Word spread quickly as what became known as the “Lincoln protocol” proved effective at treating addiction. The Puerto Rican and Black communities who made up a large population of the South Bronx increasingly sought help from Lincoln Detox. However, Lincoln’s acupuncture program proved controversial among providers trained in Western medicine and skeptical of traditional techniques. Medical lobbyists campaigned successfully for laws that made acupuncture illegal unless performed by a medical doctor - few of whom believed in or were trained in the art.

After all, the groundbreaking program was seen as a threat to the government. Lincoln Detox was providing effective care that the state was not providing, and it was all being done by radicals. The political struggles of staying in line with the law, maintaining funding and avoiding getting shut down by local and hospital police were all challenges that eventually proved to be obstacles too difficult to overcome and the program was dismantled in 1979.

Recent research has found that acupuncture still remains successful today and is being integrated into medical practices. The Lincoln Detox program paved the way for integrative medicine and health care practitioners around the globe have benefitted from their legacy. However, when it was a method used by political revolutionaries it could not continue to coexist with the state. The Black Panther Party and Young Lords had no intention of compromising their beliefs. In order to suppress the threat of opposition, the immediate solution for the government was to bury Lincoln Detox. Although it was an effective treatment program for patients struggling with drug addiction, the Black Panther Party and the Young Lords had political goals that clashed with the state and this ideological disconnect is why the program did not remain in place.

A lesson that sticks from Lincoln Detox’s story is that revolutionary ideas can help save lives. This detox program led by two rebel groups helped solve the issue of how to treat drug addiction and this success is not diminished by their short-lived existence as a treatment facility. Radical thinking led to growth in the healthcare community due to the common goal of a few rebel groups: to provide detoxification. Although they provided innovative medical treatment, there was a lack of government support for Lincoln Detox because of the perceived threat of the rebel groups gaining political legitimacy. It will be important for leaders to remember that if the focus is put on considering others as adversaries, only the negative implications from each others' actions will be seen and there will be a failure to consider the good that comes from

collaboration. Encouraging groups to work together despite differences, especially within the government, is essential in creating long-lasting and creative solutions to problems with which the world faces.
