Libya: Past Traumas and an Uncertain Future

By Quint Nigro and Soham Basu

Since 2011, Libya has been embroiled in civil war, with several international actors exerting influence and pressure. These wars are largely a consequence of Libya’s historical traumas, regional instability, and a decades-long dictatorship. This piece is designed to provide a simple, well-sourced, and accessible introduction to the current situation in Libya, its history, and potential futures.

History

Pre-Gaddafi

Modern Libya is a very new concept. The territory that would become Libya was integrated into the Ottoman Empire in 1835 as Tripoli. Before this, it was ruled by a series of regional governments in relative instability mired in fighting between different warring factions, tribes, and cities. Some of these groups even fought with the United States during the Barbary Wars.

With the signing of the Treaty of Ouchy in 1912, the Ottoman Empire ceded Tripolitania and Cyrenaica (Western and Eastern parts of modern Libya) to Italy. Italy consolidated these colonies into Italian North Africa. After World War II, control of these colonies was handed to the British, who appointed Idris as-Senussi the ruler of Libya as they left in 1951. Eight years later, the West’s interest in Libya rekindled as massive oil deposits were discovered throughout the young nation.

Idris was mostly interested in governing Cyrenaica, however, there was a lack of a unified Libyan identity up to this point in history. Idris was unable to build a singular Libyan identity. Later, Pan-Arabs were successful in building a Libyan identity through their regional solidarity. After a series of weak efforts at promoting a centralized Libyan identity around the monarchy, Idris as-Senussi would be overthrown in a 1969 coup by the Free Officers Movement. The Movement was led by Muammar Gaddafi, Libyan nationalist and dictator until 2011.

The First Civil War & the NTC

The Gaddafi regime collapsed in 2011 amid the then-surging Arab Spring protests. The grievances that the people, and especially the Libyan elite, had were brewing for a long time and can be traced back to an event that remains shrouded in intrigue: the Abu Salim prison massacre.

Allegedly, 1,200 people were killed by security forces in this government-led killing. Family members of the alleged victims took to the streets, taking part in protests that eventually developed into the Libyan rebellion. Forces with the National Transition Council captured Gaddafi with U.S. and French aid, and he was likely killed soon thereafter. The National Transition Council then tried to create a government to succeed the Gaddafi regime.

The Transition Council, which secured power in 2011, would ultimately collapse in 2014. For a variety of reasons it failed to build a cohesive state apparatus. Chief among the reasons for decay included: atrophy of state institutions, corruption, internal ideological divisions, a lack of national unity within the population, and foreign patronage of many militias.

Gaddafi’s Legacy

Gaddafi is and will remain a controversial figure. On one hand, Gaddafi helped Libya become a high-development country, with a 74+ year old average life expectancy, a literacy rate of almost 90%, and some of the highest regional gender equality. Libya had a lower rate of poverty than the Netherlands up to the point that the Gaddafi regime collapsed in 2011. Gaddafi also dug the Great Man-Made River, the largest irrigation project ever completed; it continues to be a major source of water for Libyans today.

More controversially, Gaddafi funded various paramilitary organizations across the world, including the Irish Republican Army, the Communist Party of the Philippines, the Free Aceh Movement, the Palestinian Liberation Organization, the Shining Path, and the Euskati Ta Askatasuna, and many more. It seems that Gaddafi’s criteria for supporting these movements was their generally anti-Western sentiment and little else.

The positive contributions Gaddafi made to Libya cannot distract from the fact that Muammar Gaddafi was a disturbed and violent dictator. According to allegations published in 2011, he was a serial rapist of teenage girls, guardsmen, and other lower ranking government officials. Gaddafi led Libya into several wars, including the Toyota War and a war with Egypt. Gaddafi also created the Islamic Legion, a Pan-Arab and Pan-Islamic paramilitary group that persecuted Black and Christian Libyans. He also engaged in standard dictator fare, such as censoring the media, detaining dissenters, and suppressing protest.

In modern day Libya, both the traumas and the boons left behind by Gaddafi are everywhere. Gaddafi explicitly centralized power around himself while undermining the capacity of Libya’s institutions to carry on without him. Gaddafi’s political legacy may have contributed to the instability that persists today, but he also left Libya with a large amount of its public and oil infrastructure, which are responsible for much of the money that comes into the country.

Basis of Civil War and Controversies

After the 2014 fracture, Libya was again partitioned. Many factions rose and fell, including an Islamic State faction, which made the civil war a three-way contest for a time. The two major players today are the Government of National Accord (GNA), based in Tripoli, and the Libyan National Army (LNA), which fights for the House of Representatives (HoR) and is based in Tobruk.

The LNA and GNA represent two sides of the divide between Middle Eastern and North African states. The GNA could be called a Parliamentary Islamist government (not akin to Islamic extremist non-state actors, but a nominally parliamentary government with many political parties and a political mainstream that adheres to loosely Islamic principles). The GNA enjoys the support of Turkey, the United States, and the United Nations. It is also the official, internationally recognized government of Libya.

The LNA, meanwhile, is a nationalist, militarist dictatorship. Consolidated under Khalifa Haftar, the LNA features a strong sense of nationalism, weak democratic institutions and a focus on the military as a central political, social, and cultural fixture like Bashar al-Assad’s Syria or Abdel Fattah el-Sisi’s Egypt. The LNA enjoys the support of the United Arab Emirates, Saudi Arabia, France, Egypt and Sudan, who have all committed to supplying the LNA with various levels of aid–from light arms funding to sending thousands of troops to fight on the field.

After years of fighting, the conflict between the LNA and GNA culminated in 2019 with the LNA Tripoli Offensive. LNA forces were successful in capturing Sirte but did not capture Tripoli. In the wake of this offensive, in January of 2020, both sides agreed to negotiate a ceasefire and move towards peace at the Berlin Conference.

In June 2021, both sides agreed to meet to negotiate again, though the executives refused to actually speak with each other. Despite the tense and uncertain process that lays ahead, the peace process is a light at the end of the tunnel for Libyans for the first time in a decade. Elections are slated for December 2021.

The following is a quick breakdown of the events that preceded the current situation:

First Civil War – In the upheaval of the Arab Spring, rebel forces overthrow Muammar Gaddafi’s 34-year dictatorship in October 2011 after eight months of fighting.

Second Civil War – After elections in 2014, the House of Representatives rejects the results of national elections. Fighting breaks out between the House of Representatives and the Government of National Accord.

Peace Process and Upcoming Elections – In October 2020, the HoR and GNA agreed to a permanent ceasefire that has held thus far. In March 2021, a unity government was formed and general elections are planned for December 2021.

Although the situation in Libya is delicate and volatile, some of the actors that play significant roles in the current situation include:

General National Accord – Government based in Tripoli (Western Libya) backed by the United Nations and the international community, generally thought to belong to the “Political Islam” school of thought.

House of Representatives – Government based in Tobruk (Eastern Libya) under the aegis of Khalifa Haftar and the Libyan National Army. The HoR uses the Libyan National Army (LNA) to conduct operations and is generally perceived as more autocratic than the GNA.

Government of National Unity – A provisional government formed in March 2021 as a result of the ongoing Libyan Political Dialogue Forum. This government marks an agreement between the main belligerents of the Libyan Civil War, the GNA and HoR.

Khalifa Haftar – A member of the Libyan armed forces since the beginning of his political career, Haftar took part in the overthrow of Muammar Gaddafi in 2011 before splintering with the main party. Haftar has been the de facto leader of the HoR and the LNA.

The Libyan Civil War has not been a war fought by Libyans alone. Across the Mediterranean Sea, Europe, and the Middle East, many states and governmental bodies have participated in the conflict in order to shape post-war Libya:

Saudi Arabia – Supportive of the HoR, thought to be mostly interested in countering Turkish efforts.

United Arab Emirates – Supportive of the HoR, wishes to increase the viability of Libyan oil fields and gain influence over their output.

Egypt – Supportive of the HoR due to concerns over immigration flows and radicalism. Egypt is a direct neighbor of Libya, with which it shares a 1,115 kilometer border.

Turkey – Supports the GNA and sent troops to Tripoli despite the UN embargo. Has vested interest in gaining leverage over Libyan oil exports.

Qatar – Supports the GNA and wishes to see Libya become a more viable political and economic partner.

Russia – Supports the HoR and has purportedly sent troops to Tobruk. Has vested interest in Libya’s oil fields.

Italy – Within the E.U. bloc and recognizes the GNA as the government of Libya, but Italian leaders have justified supporting the HoR to stem refugee flows.

France – Within the E.U. bloc and supports the GNA, but French leaders have justified supporting the HoR to stem refugee flows. France has also allegedly sent troops to Tobruk.

Germany – Leads the mediation effort between the GNA and HoR through a series of conferences in Berlin.

European Union – Officially supportive of the Tripoli-based GNA government.

International Influence

As much as the Libyan Civil War has been a battleground for Libyans, the diplomatic tug-of-war that has taken place behind the scenes has reached from Washington to Rome, from Ankara to Riyadh, and beyond. Foreign states and their interests have had an incalculably large effect on both the outcome and duration of the war and should be considered in the context of the conflict.

The West

As with many other events in the Middle East-North Africa region, the influence of the United States and NATO is significant. NATO supported anti-Gaddafi rebels during the first civil war, even conducting the initial airstrike that led to the death of the dictator. There has been a less active military effort since the fall of Gaddafi, however; now these parties mostly seek to exert a political influence. Italy, in particular, as well as other NATO and E.U. nations have been pressuring the GNA and the HoR to reach a peace settlement. Italy in particular has taken in a large number of refugees from Libya, a process that has been controversial domestically, with some going so far as to call it a “refugee crisis.” Some Italian politicians have gone so far as to come out in support of the HoR because they believe that the HoR’s central power would do a better job at preventing people from leaving the country.

Western governments like the E.U. and U.S. also have a vested interest in the stabilization of Libyan oil exports. Libya exported almost 1.3 million barrels of oil per day in 2010 and exported significantly less after the breakout of the war. For context, in 2010, there were 86 million barrels produced daily, which meant that Libya’s exports accounted for more than 1% of the total global daily oil export. Though Libya is now producing over one million barrels every day, an unstable Libya means an unstable export rate. It should be noted that the West is not unique in its interest in Libyan oil.

The Near East

Libya feels the pulse of the Middle East and is torn between the different agendas of external states. The Egyptians, Saudis, and Emiratis all support the HoR. Though their reasons are different, they throw their weight behind the HoR so that it might have a more advantageous position in the peace process. The Emiratis are interested in gaining influence in the now stabilizing oil fields, the Saudis want to oppose Turkish influence, and the Egyptians want a strongman neighbor to stem the growth of extremism and to prevent Libyans from coming to Egypt.

The GNA, meanwhile, enjoys the support of Turkey, which actively deployed mercenaries to Libya to fight on the GNA’s behalf, and Qatar, which wishes for Libya to be a more friendly and active economic partner. Though the GNA sees less variety of support in the Middle East, the partners that they do have, especially Turkey, have been far more willing to devote resources and even manpower to influencing the conflict in Libya than the HoR’s allies.

All of the Middle Eastern nations share concerns about Libyan oil production.

Russia

Russia is another foreign state involved in influencing Libyan affairs. They, like the West, have an interest in Libyan oil production and influence over it but see little value in the GNA, instead opting to back Haftar and the HoR. They have been a strong diplomatic ally and have allegedly sent arms and mercenaries to Libya.

It should also be noted that Russian interests have similar concerns about oil issues as outlined in the “The West” section above.

Conclusion

Libya’s tumultuous history, politics, internal divisions, natural resources and foreign influences all affect its contemporary situation. To properly understand the country one must take into account all the competing interests internally and externally along with the history of the country. Prospects for peace and elections inspire hope and optimism, but history shows that a cautious approach must be taken in every step forward. The complexity of the issue shows the intractability of history in modern conflicts and the often detrimental effects of foreign actors on individual nation-states. Elections in December 2021 will be a key test for Libya as it emerges from the throngs of the Civil War. For the people of Libya, whose generations have lived under war or dictatorship, the prospects of relative calm are welcome. Analysts must continue to watch the region, its neighbors, and internal actors to try and preserve peace.
